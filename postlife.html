Life Post WW2

Alan Turing was elected as a member of the Royal Society of London in March 1951, in honor to his major contributions, including his role in shortening World War II by over two years through his code-breaking machine. However, in March 1952, he was convicted of "Gross Indecency," a term that referred to homosexuality, which was illegal in Britain at the time. As a result, he was sentenced to 12 months of hormone therapy, which ultimately banned him from working for the Government Communications Headquarters (GCHQ), the British post-war code-breaking center. Despite these challenges, Turing continued his groundbreaking work on artificial life, publishing “The Chemical Basis of Morphogenesis” in 1952, where he detailed his research on the development of form and pattern in living organisms. Tragically, just as his new findings gained popularity, he was found dead in his bed, having been poisoned by cyanide. Authorities initially suspected suicide; however, the lack of a clear motive raised doubts, particularly given that the his hormone therapy had ended over a year before he commited suicide, and Turing was reported to be in a great state of mind at the time. Some people also thought that the hormone treatment may have affected his mental health, while others theorized that he was murdered by the government due to the amount of knowledge of sensitive information he had. Another possibility suggested that he accidentally inhaled toxic fumes from cyanide in his laboratory. Ultimately, the cause of Turing’s death remain unknown, and the true reason may never be known.
